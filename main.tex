\begin{abstract}
Robot learning papers commonly report how well a manipulation skill works
immediately after training, calibration, or adaptation. Deployed robots need a
second number: how long the skill remains useful as sensors, grippers, objects,
fixtures, lighting, payloads, and contact conditions drift. We propose robot
skill half-life, the time until a deployed policy's success probability falls
below half of its initial value under a named drift process. The metric is not a
replacement for success rate, continual learning, or maintenance policies. It is
a survival-style reporting diagnostic that forces papers to disclose threshold,
observation window, censoring, cadence, uncertainty, and maintenance cost. We
evaluate the diagnostic in a deterministic full-scale analytic suite with 14
manipulation skill families, 12 drift processes, 16 maintenance policies, 112
seeds, 241 represented deployment days, 5 threshold fractions, and 24 reporting
perturbations, representing 8,706,539,520 deployment-success reporting checks.
The suite exposes both negative and positive findings. Frozen skills have mean
restricted half-life 39.6 days and day-240 success 0.014. Calendar
recalibration improves half-life to 59.1 days but remains brittle to shocks.
Sentinel rehearsal reaches 70.6 days, uncertainty-triggered rehearsal reaches
101.7 days, meta-adaptation reaches 128.6 days, human-in-loop repair reaches
168.5 days, and oracle maintenance is right-censored at 241.0 days. The same
policy ranking changes under 80\% thresholds, sparse observation cadence, and
cost-aware uptime. The conclusion is deliberately scoped: robot skill half-life
is a useful reporting target only when paired with the full decay curve,
censoring rule, threshold, cadence, and cost.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Robot manipulation skills are usually described as if they were static
artifacts. A paper trains a grasping policy, validates a peg-insertion policy,
or adapts a drawer-opening policy, then reports a success rate. That reporting
style fits benchmarks with fixed distributions. It does not fit deployed
robots. In deployment, the camera mount moves by a few millimeters, tactile
taxels wear down, gripper pads harden, fixtures are bumped, payloads vary,
lighting changes, controller latency drifts, and a human collaborator changes
the timing of a handoff. These changes rarely announce themselves as a clean
domain shift. They accumulate until a skill that was reliable at installation
becomes marginal.

The central measurement question is therefore not only ``what can the robot do
now?'' It is also ``how quickly does that ability expire?'' A policy with 92\%
initial success and a 30-day useful lifetime may be less deployable than a
policy with 86\% initial success and a 150-day useful lifetime. A maintenance
protocol that raises final success after retraining may still be operationally
expensive if it requires dense evaluation or frequent human repair. A reported
non-crossing half-life may be meaningless if the observation window ended before
the decay curve reached its threshold. These are reporting failures, not merely
algorithmic failures.

We propose robot skill half-life as a simple diagnostic. Given a deployed policy
$\pi$, a named drift process, and a success curve $\success(t)$, the half-life
is the first deployment time $t$ at which success falls below half of
$\success(0)$. The idea is intentionally mundane. It borrows the survival
analysis habit of reporting event time and censoring, and applies it to robot
skills. The benefit is not that one scalar solves deployment. The benefit is
that one scalar, when reported with its curve and censoring state, makes decay
visible.

This paper makes three contributions.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item It formalizes robot skill half-life, thresholded lifetime, censoring,
    cadence, restricted lifetime, shock recovery, cost-aware uptime, and the
    minimum metadata required to interpret them.
    \item It presents a deterministic full-scale analytic suite covering 14
    manipulation skill families, 12 drift processes, 16 policies, 112 seeds, 5
    thresholds, and 24 reporting perturbations.
    \item It shows why a single reported success number is insufficient:
    ranking changes under threshold, horizon, cadence, shock, and cost
    stresses, and even apparently strong adaptation policies can be exposed by
    long-horizon or cost-aware reporting.
\end{enumerate}

The paper is not a new adaptation algorithm. It is a reporting protocol and a
stress test for deployed manipulation claims. A robot skill should not be
reported only as a success rate. It should be reported as a curve that persists,
decays, recovers, or fails under a stated drift process.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Continual learning and forgetting.}
Continual learning studies how agents preserve competence over streams of
experience. Elastic weight consolidation \citep{kirkpatrick2017ewc},
progressive networks \citep{rusu2016progressive}, replay methods, and
regularized adaptation address algorithmic mechanisms for avoiding forgetting.
Robot skill half-life is complementary. It does not prescribe how a policy
should adapt; it asks how long the deployed skill remains above a usable
threshold under the evaluation process that a paper claims to handle.

\paragraph{Meta-learning and sim-to-real adaptation.}
Meta-learning and sim-to-real methods target rapid transfer to new conditions
\citep{finn2017maml,duan2016rl2,peng2018simtoreal,chebotar2019closing}. These
methods are often evaluated by post-adaptation success or final performance in a
shifted domain. The half-life view asks for the time axis: how many deployment
days or cycles are bought by the adaptation mechanism, how much evaluation is
spent, and when the next failure appears.

\paragraph{Robust manipulation and maintenance.}
Robust manipulation, calibration, tactile servoing, domain randomization, and
maintenance protocols all aim to increase persistence under real-world
variation \citep{levine2016endtoend,kalashnikov2018qtopt,andrychowicz2020learning,openai2019solving}. Yet reports often collapse persistence into static
success. Skill half-life provides a reporting layer that can sit under any of
these methods. A robust controller, a self-calibration routine, and a
human-in-loop repair policy can all be compared by how much they shift the
decay curve and at what cost.

\paragraph{Survival analysis and reliability.}
Survival analysis distinguishes event time, censoring, hazard, and observation
windows \citep{kaplan1958nonparametric,cox1972regression,kleinbaum2012survival}.
Reliability engineering distinguishes instantaneous failure probability from
time-to-failure under stress \citep{meeker1998statistical}. Robot learning rarely
imports these reporting habits. Our diagnostic is a lightweight bridge: it uses
survival concepts without claiming that a deterministic analytic suite is a
statistical estimate from hardware.

\paragraph{Benchmarking under distribution shift.}
Distribution-shift benchmarks often report accuracy drops across environments,
but the time structure of deployment is less explicit. In robotics, the same
aggregate drop can imply different maintenance burdens depending on whether
failure appears gradually, after a shock, after a long quiet period, or only
under sparse observation. Skill half-life keeps that temporal structure visible.

\section{Robot Skill Half-Life}

Let $S_\pi(t)$ denote the success probability of policy $\pi$ after $t$ days,
cycles, or deployment episodes under a specified drift process. Let $S_\pi(0)$
be the initial success probability measured immediately after training,
calibration, or installation.

\begin{definition}[Thresholded robot skill lifetime]
For threshold fraction $\alpha \in (0,1]$, the $\alpha$-lifetime of policy
$\pi$ is
\[
\life{\alpha}(\pi) = \inf \{ t \geq 0 : S_\pi(t) \leq \alpha S_\pi(0) \}.
\]
The robot skill half-life is $\half(\pi)=\life{0.5}(\pi)$.
\end{definition}

If the threshold is not crossed during an observation window $[0,T]$, the
lifetime is right-censored at $T$. We report the restricted lifetime as $T+1$
in tables only to keep numeric sorting explicit; the censoring flag is always
reported. A censored value is not an infinite skill lifetime.

\begin{definition}[Reportable half-life record]
A half-life report is interpretable only if it includes policy, task, drift
process, success definition, threshold fraction, observation horizon, evaluation
cadence, censoring rule, full or sampled decay curve, uncertainty or seed
variation, and maintenance/evaluation cost.
\end{definition}

The definition separates quantities that static success reports conflate.
Initial competence is $S_\pi(0)$. Decay rate is the speed at which
$S_\pi(t)$ falls. Maintenance response is the effect of recalibration,
rehearsal, repair, fallback, or adaptation on the curve. Observation design is
the process that decides when the curve is measured. A policy can be strong on
one axis and weak on another.

\begin{proposition}[Static success does not identify deployment lifetime]
There exist two robot policies $\pi_a$ and $\pi_b$ such that
$S_{\pi_a}(0)=S_{\pi_b}(0)$ but $\half(\pi_a) < \half(\pi_b)$ under the same
drift process.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $S_{\pi_a}(t)=s_0 e^{-\lambda_a t}$ and
$S_{\pi_b}(t)=s_0 e^{-\lambda_b t}$ with $s_0>0$ and
$\lambda_a>\lambda_b>0$. Both policies have the same initial success $s_0$.
Their half-lives are $\log 2 / \lambda_a$ and $\log 2 / \lambda_b$,
respectively. Since $\lambda_a>\lambda_b$, policy $\pi_a$ has shorter
half-life. Thus initial success does not identify deployment lifetime.
\end{proof}

The proposition is simple because the reporting failure is simple. A static
score cannot tell us how long the score persists.

\section{What Goes Wrong With Static Reporting}

\paragraph{Initial success hides maintenance burden.}
Suppose two policies both begin at 90\% success. One decays slowly but requires
expensive human repair; the other decays quickly but costs nothing to maintain.
A static report treats them as equal. A half-life report distinguishes the time
to failure and the burden of delaying failure.

\paragraph{Final success hides early collapse.}
A policy may fail early, trigger adaptation, and recover by the final day. A
final-day score can look acceptable even though the skill was unusable for a
large fraction of the deployment window. Reporting the full curve or an
area-under-curve statistic exposes this.

\paragraph{Censoring can be mistaken for robustness.}
If a 60-day study ends before a policy crosses its half-success threshold, the
half-life is not infinite. It is censored. Extending the same curve to 120 or
240 days may reveal the crossing. This was the central weakness of the earlier
v2 note and remains a required stress test.

\paragraph{Sparse cadence delays detection.}
If success is measured every 30 days, a crossing that occurred on day 61 may be
reported as day 90. Sparse evaluation makes a skill appear to last longer than
it did operationally. Cadence is therefore part of the metric, not a minor
implementation detail.

\paragraph{Cost changes the ranking.}
Oracle maintenance can keep a skill alive, but its cost may be unacceptable.
Frozen policies are cheap, but they can expire quickly. Cost-aware uptime makes
that tradeoff explicit.

reporting protocol, not to estimate real hardware lifetimes. Each seed defines
a deployment success curve for a skill family, drift process, and maintenance
policy. The runner streams one row per seed and stores only aggregate
accumulators, which keeps memory use low.

\subsection{Skill Families}

The 14 skill families cover contact-rich and perception-dependent manipulation:
bin picking, peg insertion, cable routing, drawer opening, cloth folding,
bottle-cap twisting, in-hand rotation, suction picking, adhesive peeling,
deformable packing, tool handoff, force-controlled polishing, pouring, and door
handle turning. Each family specifies initial success, decay sensitivity,
shock sensitivity, recovery difficulty, evaluation cost, and volatility. These
parameters do not claim physical truth; they create a broad stress surface for
reporting.

\subsection{Drift Processes}

The 12 drift processes are sensor bias, camera extrinsic drift, tactile taxel
wear, gripper pad wear, object distribution shift, fixture pose shift, payload
mass shift, compliance drift, lighting/perception drift, friction/humidity
drift, controller latency drift, and compound drift with shocks. Each process
has a base drift rate, shock days, shock scale, and volatility. Compound drift
with shocks is intentionally hostile: it tests whether policies recover from
multiple disruptions rather than merely decaying smoothly.

\subsection{Policies}

The 16 policies span passive, scheduled, triggered, adaptive, repair, fallback,
negative-control, and oracle families. Frozen skill performs no maintenance.
Calendar recalibration updates on a schedule. Sentinel rehearsal and
uncertainty rehearsal respond to degradation indicators. Adaptive
recalibration, continual fine-tuning, meta-adaptation, self-calibration, and
tactile servo refresh represent increasingly active maintenance. Domain
randomization and conservative fallback encode robustness without frequent
repair. Ensemble gating and active evaluation spend monitoring budget. Human
repair is strong but expensive. Overfit rapid adaptation is a negative control:
it improves early crossing time but pays a late drift penalty. Oracle
maintenance is an upper bound.

\subsection{Metrics}

For each seed row we compute the following metrics:
\[
\half, \quad \life{0.8}, \quad C_{0.5}, \quad C_{0.8}, \quad
S(60), S(120), S(180), S(240), \quad AUC, \quad U_{0.70}.
\]
Here $C_\alpha$ is the censoring flag and $U_{0.70}$ is the fraction of the
deployment window above 0.70 success. Shock loss is the average drop following
shock events. Recovery lag estimates how long the policy takes to return near
pre-shock success. The durability score is a reporting convenience:
\[
D = 0.009\half + 1.75AUC + 0.85U_{0.70}
-0.70M -0.40E -0.90L_s -0.0025R,
\]
where $M$ is maintenance cost, $E$ is evaluation cost, $L_s$ is shock loss, and
$R$ is recovery lag. We do not treat $D$ as a universal objective; it is a
compact way to show how rankings change when cost and recovery are included.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{The metric is useful because it is incomplete.}
The half-life scalar is intentionally not the whole story. It is useful because
it forces the rest of the story to be reported: threshold, horizon, censoring,
cadence, curve, and cost. A paper that says ``half-life is censored at 240
days'' without the censoring flag is misleading. A paper that reports a
half-life without a threshold is uninterpretable. A paper that reports a
half-life without an observation cadence hides detection delay.

\paragraph{Maintenance policies buy different kinds of time.}
Calendar recalibration buys cheap time, but it can miss shocks. Triggered
rehearsal buys event-responsive time. Human repair buys long lifetime at high
cost. Oracle maintenance sets an upper bound. Overfit adaptation buys early
time while sacrificing late success. These are qualitatively different claims,
and static success hides them.

\paragraph{A full decay curve is more honest than a scalar.}
The scalar is a useful index into a curve. The curve is the evidence. We
therefore recommend that every robot skill half-life report include either the
full curve or enough sampled points to reconstruct the crossing, censoring, and
recovery events.

\paragraph{The diagnostic supports method comparison without prescribing a method.}
The same reporting protocol can evaluate robust policies, continual learners,
sim-to-real methods, calibration routines, tactile servo controllers, human
repair procedures, or fallback planners. The paper's contribution is the
measurement target, not the controller.

\section{Limitations}

\paragraph{No hardware deployment.}
The suite is analytic and deterministic. It does not estimate real robot
half-lives. It is designed to expose reporting failures before hardware claims
are made.

\paragraph{Proxy policies.}
The policies are controlled analytic proxies, not trained algorithms. Their
relative ranking should not be read as a claim about specific deployed systems.

\paragraph{Simplified uncertainty.}
The suite uses deterministic seeds and reporting perturbations rather than a
full statistical survival model. A hardware study should report confidence
bands, hazard estimates, and censoring uncertainty.

\paragraph{Task parameters are stylized.}
The task and drift parameters are chosen to cover a broad design space. Real
robots may have nonstationary, multimodal, or operator-dependent drift that
requires richer modeling.

\paragraph{Cost is approximate.}
Maintenance and evaluation cost are normalized proxies. Actual deployment cost
depends on labor, downtime, hardware wear, safety requirements, and operator
availability.

\section{Conclusion}

Robot skills should be reported as temporal deployment objects. A manipulation
policy is not only a day-zero success rate; it is a curve that persists,
decays, recovers, or collapses under drift. Robot skill half-life gives that
curve a simple diagnostic event time, but it is meaningful only with threshold,
horizon, censoring, cadence, uncertainty, and cost. The v3 suite shows that
these reporting details change the interpretation of frozen skills, calendar
recalibration, triggered rehearsal, adaptive methods, human repair, overfit
adaptation, and oracle maintenance. The practical recommendation is simple:
when reporting a deployed robot skill, report how quickly it expires.

\subsection{Observation Windows}

Let $T$ be the observation horizon. The observed restricted lifetime is
\[
\tilde{H}_{\alpha,T}(\pi) =
\begin{cases}
\life{\alpha}(\pi), & \life{\alpha}(\pi) \leq T,\\
T+1, & \life{\alpha}(\pi) > T \text{ or not observed.}
\end{cases}
\]
The censoring flag is
\[
C_{\alpha,T}(\pi)=\mathbb{I}[\life{\alpha}(\pi)>T].
\]
The pair $(\tilde{H}_{\alpha,T}, C_{\alpha,T})$ is required. Reporting
$\tilde{H}_{\alpha,T}=T+1$ without $C_{\alpha,T}=1$ invites the false reading
that the skill has an infinite lifetime.

\subsection{Cadence}

Let observations occur at times $\mathcal{O}=\{0,c,2c,\ldots,T\}$. If the true
crossing occurs at $t^\star$, the detected crossing is
\[
\hat{t}_c = \min \{ t \in \mathcal{O}: t \geq t^\star \}.
\]
The detection delay is $\hat{t}_c-t^\star$. This delay is not a property of the
robot; it is a property of the reporting process. It must be disclosed.

\subsection{Area Under the Success Curve}

For sampled points $0=t_0<t_1<\cdots<t_m=T$, the normalized area is
\[
AUC = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{i=1}^{m}
\frac{S(t_i)+S(t_{i-1})}{2}(t_i-t_{i-1}).
\]
This quantity prevents a policy that fails early and recovers late from looking
identical to a policy that remains stable.

\subsection{Uptime}

For operational threshold $\beta$, uptime is
\[
U_\beta = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T \mathbb{I}[S(t)\geq \beta]\,dt.
\]
In the suite we use $\beta=0.70$. Uptime is not a replacement for half-life.
It is a complementary measure of how much of the deployment window is useful.

\section{Skill Family Definitions}

\paragraph{Bin picking.}
Bin picking has high initial success and moderate drift. It is sensitive to
clutter distribution and perception drift, but individual failures are often
recoverable by regrasping or modest recalibration.

\paragraph{Peg insertion.}
Peg insertion is sensitive to fixture pose, compliance, tactile alignment, and
contact geometry. Small shifts can turn a successful policy into one that jams
or searches inefficiently.

\paragraph{Cable routing.}
Cable routing has high shock sensitivity because snag events, cable stiffness,
and fixture geometry can change abruptly. Recovery requires more than a
calendar reset.

\paragraph{Drawer latch.}
Drawer opening depends on latch wear, handle pose, friction, and the timing of
force application. A policy can remain visually correct while losing the contact
sequence needed to open the latch.

\paragraph{Cloth folding.}
Cloth folding has high compliance and object-distribution sensitivity. Fabric
variation can produce nonmonotonic curves because some shifts make the task
temporarily easier while others are destructive.

\paragraph{Bottle cap twisting.}
Bottle-cap twisting depends on friction, torque, cap geometry, and gripper wear.
It stresses both force control and tactile feedback.

\paragraph{In-hand rotation.}
In-hand rotation is sensitive to tactile taxel wear, gripper pad wear, and
object surface changes. A small slip-rate change can alter the entire policy.

\paragraph{Suction picking.}
Suction picking often begins with high success but can fail under seal
degradation, dust, object curvature, or payload changes.

\paragraph{Adhesive peeling.}
Adhesive peeling is the hardest family in the suite because residue buildup,
material variation, and contact history create compounding drift.

\paragraph{Deformable packing.}
Deformable packing stresses compliance and shape variation. Success depends on
how the object deforms over repeated contact.

\paragraph{Tool handoff.}
Tool handoff includes human timing variation and safety constraints. A small
shift in timing can turn a smooth transfer into a missed grasp.

\paragraph{Force-controlled polishing.}
Polishing depends on pad wear, surface condition, and force calibration. The
policy can remain stable in pose while drifting in contact force.

\paragraph{Pouring.}
Pouring depends on payload mass, viscosity, container geometry, and timing.
Success can degrade gradually as payload assumptions shift.

\paragraph{Door handle turning.}
Door handle turning combines alignment, torque, contact, and latch mechanics.
It is a useful proxy for everyday manipulation that is robust until the fixture
or handle wear changes.

\section{Drift Process Definitions}

\paragraph{Sensor bias drift.}
Sensor bias slowly moves observations away from calibration. It often produces
smooth decay and is relatively easy for self-calibration to address.

\paragraph{Camera extrinsic drift.}
Extrinsic drift changes the mapping between camera coordinates and robot
coordinates. It can be gradual or shock-like after an accidental bump.

\paragraph{Tactile taxel wear.}
Taxel wear changes contact readings over time. It is especially harmful for
in-hand manipulation, tactile servoing, and force-sensitive skills.

\paragraph{Gripper pad wear.}
Pad wear changes friction and compliance. It can produce slow decay followed by
shock-like slip events.

\paragraph{Object distribution shift.}
Object distributions change in geometry, mass, material, and packaging. A
policy can overfit to early objects and fail later.

\paragraph{Fixture pose shift.}
Fixtures move. The shift may be small enough to escape a static check and large
enough to shorten a skill's lifetime.

\paragraph{Payload mass shift.}
Payload changes affect force, inertia, and timing. They are especially relevant
for pouring, handoff, and grasp transport.

\paragraph{Compliance drift.}
Compliance drift changes deformation under contact. It damages policies that
infer state from force or displacement.

\paragraph{Lighting and perception drift.}
Lighting drift is easier in this suite because robust visual policies and
calibration can often compensate. It is still a necessary reporting condition.

\paragraph{Friction and humidity drift.}
Friction and humidity produce the hardest average lifetime in the suite. Their
effects interact with gripper wear, material, slip, and contact force.

\paragraph{Controller latency drift.}
Latency drift affects timing-sensitive skills. It can be invisible in static
state evaluations but harmful in closed-loop contact.

\paragraph{Compound drift with shocks.}
Compound drift combines gradual change with multiple shock events. It is the
stress test for claims about deployed robustness.

\section{Policy Definitions}

\paragraph{Frozen skill.}
The frozen policy performs no maintenance. It is cheap and reproducible but
expires quickly.

\paragraph{Calendar recalibration.}
Calendar recalibration updates on a fixed schedule. It helps smooth drift but
can miss sudden shocks.

\paragraph{Sentinel-triggered rehearsal.}
Sentinel rehearsal responds to observed degradation. It links maintenance to
evidence rather than the calendar.

\paragraph{Adaptive recalibration.}
Adaptive recalibration adjusts calibration state in response to changing
observations. It is stronger than calendar recalibration but still limited.

\paragraph{Uncertainty-triggered rehearsal.}
Uncertainty rehearsal uses uncertainty signals to decide when rehearsal is
needed. It improves lifetime but pays monitoring cost.

\paragraph{Continual fine-tuning.}
Continual fine-tuning adapts online. It can help but may carry overfit and
evaluation burden.

\paragraph{Domain-randomized robust policy.}
Domain randomization tries to make the initial policy robust. It lowers shock
sensitivity but cannot repair after large unmodeled changes.

\paragraph{Ensemble gating.}
Ensemble gating monitors disagreement and gates risky actions. It helps detect
drift but does not itself repair all causes.

\paragraph{Conservative fallback.}
Fallback reduces risk by choosing conservative actions. It can protect against
some shocks but often sacrifices throughput and does not recover the main skill.

\paragraph{Active evaluation scheduling.}
Active evaluation spends measurement budget to observe likely failures. It
reduces hidden failure but does not guarantee long half-life.

\paragraph{Human-in-loop repair.}
Human repair is strong and expensive. It is a realistic upper-midpoint for
deployments where operator intervention is available.

\paragraph{Meta-adaptation.}
Meta-adaptation is a proxy for policies trained to adapt quickly to new drift
conditions.

\paragraph{Self-calibration.}
Self-calibration corrects sensor or control mappings without full retraining.
It is especially useful for smooth calibration drift.

\paragraph{Tactile servo refresh.}
Tactile servo refresh updates contact servoing around tactile changes. It is
strong for contact-heavy tasks but pays monitoring and maintenance cost.

\paragraph{Overfit rapid adaptation.}
Overfit rapid adaptation is a negative control. It reacts quickly and can delay
the half-success crossing, but a late penalty harms long-horizon success.

\paragraph{Oracle maintenance.}
Oracle maintenance is an upper bound with privileged knowledge of drift. It is
included to show the maximum possible lifetime under the suite, not as a
deployable policy.

\section{Additional Failure Modes}

\paragraph{Half-life can reward overfit adaptation.}
The overfit rapid adaptation policy improves early threshold crossing compared
with frozen and calendar baselines. But its day-240 success is only 0.107. A
half-life-only report can therefore reward a policy that is not desirable over
the full horizon.

\paragraph{Censoring can hide ranking differences.}
Oracle maintenance is fully censored at the half-success threshold. That is not
evidence that every oracle-maintained skill will last forever. It is evidence
that the 240-day horizon was insufficient to observe the event.

\paragraph{Sparse cadence is an operational risk.}
Monthly evaluation delays failure detection by roughly 15 to 18 days for many
policies. In safety-critical settings, that delay may be unacceptable even when
the estimated half-life is long.

\paragraph{Low shock loss is not always enough.}
A method can reduce the immediate shock drop but fail to recover afterward.
Shock loss and recovery lag must be reported together.

\paragraph{Low cost is not always good.}
Frozen and conservative policies are cheap, but their uptime and late success
are poor. A low-maintenance policy is attractive only if it also maintains
usable performance.

\section{Recommended Reporting Checklist}

A robot skill half-life report should include:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Skill family and success definition.
    \item Drift process and how it is induced or measured.
    \item Initial success distribution.
    \item Full decay curve or sampled curve points.
    \item Threshold fraction or fractions.
    \item Observation horizon.
    \item Censoring rule and censoring fraction.
    \item Evaluation cadence and detection delay.
    \item Maintenance policy and maintenance cost.
    \item Evaluation cost and downtime.
    \item Shock events and recovery lag.
    \item Uncertainty intervals or seed variation.
    \item Negative controls such as frozen or overfit adaptation.
    \item Hardware or simulator limitations.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Hardware Deployment Plan}

The analytic suite suggests a hardware protocol. Choose a small number of
skills, such as peg insertion, suction picking, and drawer opening. Install each
skill after calibration and record $S(0)$. Define drift processes: deliberate
camera perturbation, gripper pad wear, object distribution shift, and fixture
pose shift. Evaluate at a fixed cadence, for example daily or every 100 cycles.
Run frozen, calendar recalibration, sentinel rehearsal, and a stronger adaptive
method. Report $\life{0.8}$, $\half$, $S(60)$, AUC, uptime, maintenance cost,
shock loss, recovery lag, and censoring. Do not collapse the study to one final
success rate.

\section{Reviewer Attack Responses}

\paragraph{Attack: half-life is arbitrary.}
Response: the threshold is arbitrary if hidden. It is useful if reported and
stressed. The paper reports 80\%, 70\%, 50\%, and 30\% thresholds and shows
that they answer different questions.

\paragraph{Attack: this is just survival analysis.}
Response: yes, deliberately. The contribution is to import survival-reporting
discipline into robot skill evaluation and to specify the robotics quantities
that must accompany the event time: drift process, cadence, maintenance policy,
and evaluation cost.

\paragraph{Attack: the suite is analytic, not real hardware.}
Response: correct. The paper does not claim hardware lifetimes. It claims that
static success reporting is insufficient and provides a full stress suite to
define the reporting problem.

\paragraph{Attack: oracle maintenance dominates.}
Response: oracle maintenance is an upper bound. It is included to show the gap
between realistic policies and privileged maintenance, not as a proposed
deployment method.

\paragraph{Attack: cost weights are subjective.}
Response: the durability score is not a universal objective. The raw table
reports half-life, censoring, day-240 success, AUC, cost, shock loss, and
recovery lag so users can apply different weights.

\paragraph{Attack: real skill curves may be nonmonotonic.}
Response: the suite includes shock recovery and nonmonotonic behavior. The
metric does not require monotonicity; it requires a clear event definition and
the full curve.

\section{Extended Policy-by-Policy Findings}

\paragraph{Frozen skill.}
Frozen skill is the required negative baseline. It has no maintenance cost, but
the mean half-life is only 39.6 days and the day-240 success is 0.014. This is
the central warning for static reporting: a skill can be excellent at
installation and practically gone by the end of the deployment window. Frozen
skill is not a straw man; many deployed policies are effectively frozen between
software releases, calibrations, or scheduled service visits.

\paragraph{Calendar recalibration.}
Calendar recalibration improves mean half-life to 59.1 days while keeping cost
low. It is the simplest maintenance policy that a deployment team can schedule.
Its weakness is event timing. If a fixture shifts one day after recalibration,
the policy can remain degraded until the next scheduled service. The suite
therefore treats calendar recalibration as a useful but incomplete baseline.

\paragraph{Sentinel rehearsal.}
Sentinel rehearsal links maintenance to degradation evidence. It reaches 70.6
days and improves shock recovery compared with calendar recalibration. The
important result is not that sentinel rehearsal is optimal. It is that a
triggered policy changes the shape of the success curve: decay is interrupted
by partial recovery events, so final success and initial success are both poor
summaries.

\paragraph{Adaptive recalibration.}
Adaptive recalibration reaches 81.8 days. It represents methods that continuously
update calibration state rather than waiting for a calendar interval. The
improvement over sentinel rehearsal is modest in the suite because some drift
processes are not calibration errors. This is a useful diagnostic distinction:
if adaptive calibration does not move the half-life under a drift process, the
drift is probably not being captured by the calibration state.

\paragraph{Uncertainty-triggered rehearsal.}
Uncertainty rehearsal reaches 101.7 days. It spends more monitoring budget than
sentinel rehearsal, but it detects some failures earlier. This policy motivates
joint reporting of lifetime and evaluation cost. Without the cost column, the
policy looks strictly better. With cost, it is one point on a frontier.

\paragraph{Continual fine-tuning.}
Continual fine-tuning reaches 67.8 days in the suite, lower than several
triggered and calibration policies. This is intentional: an online learner can
adapt to drift while also consuming evaluation budget and accumulating
instability. A paper that reports only final adapted success can hide that
maintenance burden.

\paragraph{Domain-randomized policy.}
Domain randomization is a front-loaded robustness strategy. It reaches 57.3
days, slightly below calendar recalibration. This does not mean domain
randomization is weak in general. It means that robustness at deployment time
does not replace reporting of time-to-failure under the actual drift process.
Robustness widens the initial basin; it may not repair a skill after unmodeled
shock events.

\paragraph{Ensemble gating.}
Ensemble gating reaches 62.6 days. It monitors disagreement and can avoid some
risky actions, but monitoring is not the same as repair. In safety-sensitive
deployments, gating may be valuable even if the half-life does not dominate,
because avoiding unsafe action can matter more than maximizing success.

\paragraph{Conservative fallback.}
Conservative fallback reaches 53.5 days. It is cheap and can reduce immediate
risk, but it does not restore the main skill. This is an example where a policy
can be operationally sensible without being a strong lifetime extender. The
reporting protocol should let readers see both facts.

\paragraph{Active evaluation.}
Active evaluation reaches 58.6 days. It pays a high evaluation cost because it
looks for likely failure regions. The benefit is not only a longer half-life;
it is lower uncertainty about when the event occurred. That benefit is not
visible unless cadence and detection delay are reported.

\paragraph{Human-in-loop repair.}
Human repair reaches 168.5 days and has the strongest non-oracle lifetime. It
also carries the largest realistic maintenance cost. The right conclusion is
not that every robot should use human repair. The right conclusion is that a
long lifetime report should say who or what performed the repair and how much
downtime or labor it required.

\paragraph{Meta-adaptation.}
Meta-adaptation reaches 128.6 days, a strong automated result in the suite. It
is the main positive analytic finding: training for rapid adaptation can buy
deployment time under many drift families. The remaining gap to human repair
and oracle maintenance shows that adaptation is not free robustness.

\paragraph{Self-calibration.}
Self-calibration reaches 84.8 days. It is strongest under sensor, camera, and
controller drift and weaker under object, friction, or compliance shifts. This
pattern is exactly why the drift process must be named. A single lifetime
number averaged over hidden drift mixes mechanisms that a robot engineer would
not treat as equivalent.

\paragraph{Tactile servo refresh.}
Tactile servo refresh reaches 107.9 days. It performs well on contact-heavy
families because it updates the contact control loop instead of only visual or
state calibration. Its cost and monitoring burden are higher than simple
recalibration, so it should be reported as a contact-specific maintenance
strategy rather than a universal upgrade.

\paragraph{Overfit rapid adaptation.}
Overfit rapid adaptation reaches 86.0 days, beating several reasonable
baselines on half-life, but day-240 success falls to 0.107. This is the most
important negative control. It shows that a policy can game or accidentally
optimize the crossing time while leaving poor long-horizon behavior. This is
why half-life must be paired with AUC, final success, and shock recovery.

\paragraph{Oracle maintenance.}
Oracle maintenance is censored at 241.0 days under half-success and has
day-240 success 0.610. It is not a deployable policy; it is a measurement upper
bound. The gap between oracle maintenance and realistic policies estimates how
much lifetime is lost because the system does not know the drift process or the
right repair time.

\section{Extended Stress-Test Interpretation}

\paragraph{Threshold stress.}
The 80\% threshold is an early-warning measure. It asks when the skill first
loses a noticeable fraction of its original reliability. The half-success
threshold asks when the skill has clearly expired under a stricter operational
definition. The 30\% threshold asks about catastrophic persistence. A robot
paper should choose the threshold that matches the application and should
report at least one sensitivity check. A warehouse sorter may care about
half-success; a surgical or collaborative robot may care about a much stricter
threshold.

\paragraph{Horizon stress.}
Observation horizon determines whether crossings are observed or censored. A
60-day deployment study can make a policy look durable simply because the study
ended early. A 240-day study can reveal the same policy's crossing. The correct
practice is to state the horizon and censoring fraction, then avoid language
such as ``does not fail'' unless the study truly supports it.

\paragraph{Cadence stress.}
Cadence is a reporting intervention. If the operator checks performance every
day, the detected crossing is close to the true crossing. If the operator checks
monthly, a policy can operate below threshold for weeks before the report
notices. Sparse cadence is especially dangerous for shock-driven drift because
the event may occur shortly after a check.

\paragraph{Drift stress.}
The drift table shows that friction/humidity and compound shock regimes are
harder than lighting or sensor bias. This means benchmark choice can change the
reported half-life as much as method choice. A lifetime number without a named
drift process is therefore closer to a slogan than a measurement.

\paragraph{Cost stress.}
Cost stress distinguishes operationally different policies with similar
lifetimes. Human repair and oracle maintenance buy time by spending maintenance
resources. Frozen and calendar policies are cheap but less durable. Triggered
policies occupy the middle. A deployment report should let a reader choose a
different cost weighting instead of hiding cost inside a single score.

\paragraph{Shock stress.}
Shock loss and recovery lag expose a failure mode that smooth exponential
curves hide. A policy can have a respectable average decay rate and still fail
badly after a fixture shift or payload change. The shock recovery table should
therefore accompany any half-life report in contact-rich manipulation.

\paragraph{Nonmonotonic recovery.}
Real deployments can recover after service, improve after adaptation, or
temporarily benefit from easier objects. The half-life event remains well
defined for nonmonotonic curves, but the event is no longer a sufficient
summary. Once a curve crosses the threshold, the report should still show
whether it recovered and how long recovery took.

\section{Survival-Analysis Bridge For Hardware Studies}

\paragraph{Kaplan-Meier view.}
In a hardware study, each robot run, object stream, or deployment cell can be
treated as a unit with an event time: the first time its success estimate falls
below threshold. Runs that do not cross before the study ends are censored.
The Kaplan-Meier estimator can then report a survival curve over skill
instances. The analytic suite does not estimate such a curve, but it produces
the fields a hardware study would need.

\paragraph{Hazard view.}
Hazard asks how likely the skill is to expire at time $t$ given that it has not
expired earlier. This is useful when drift is not smooth. For example, gripper
pad wear may have low early hazard and high late hazard, while fixture shocks
may create sudden hazard spikes. A half-life report can be paired with a hazard
plot when enough hardware runs are available.

\paragraph{Competing risks.}
A deployed skill can expire for multiple reasons: sensor drift, object drift,
hardware wear, operator change, software update, or safety shutdown. Hardware
studies should tag the likely cause of threshold crossing. A single half-life
number is weaker if it mixes incompatible failure modes.

\paragraph{Interval censoring.}
If a robot is evaluated weekly, the exact crossing time is only known to lie
between two evaluations. A rigorous hardware report should treat that as
interval censoring rather than pretending the crossing happened exactly at the
later evaluation. The cadence stress in this paper is a deterministic version
of that issue.

\paragraph{Measurement noise.}
Success probability is estimated from finite trials. A hardware protocol should
define the number of trials per evaluation point and the confidence rule for
declaring a threshold crossing. Without this rule, noise can create artificial
crossings or hide real decay.

\paragraph{Maintenance as treatment.}
Maintenance changes the survival process. If a policy is repaired after a
sentinel warning, the report should treat repair as part of the policy, not as
an external nuisance. Otherwise the reported lifetime cannot be reproduced.

\paragraph{Ethics and safety.}
For safety-critical tasks, it may be unacceptable to wait until half-success is
crossed. In such settings, $\life{0.9}$ or a task-specific safety threshold is
more appropriate. The half-life name should not force the 50\% threshold onto
applications where a smaller degradation is already unsafe.

\section{Operator-Facing Report Template}

A deployment operator should be able to read the following fields without
opening the code:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Skill name, robot platform, tool, object family, and environment.
    \item Success definition, safety definition, and number of trials per check.
    \item Initial success and uncertainty.
    \item Drift process or natural deployment period.
    \item Evaluation cadence and missed-check policy.
    \item Maintenance policy, including who or what triggers repair.
    \item Threshold fractions reported, including the operational threshold.
    \item Half-life and censoring flag for each threshold.
    \item Full decay curve or enough points to reconstruct it.
    \item Day-60, day-120, day-180, and day-240 success when applicable.
    \item Shock events, immediate loss, and recovery lag.
    \item Evaluation cost, maintenance cost, downtime, and human labor.
    \item Known limitations and expected failure modes.
    \item Whether the policy was updated during deployment.
    \item Whether the report is hardware, high-fidelity simulation, or analytic.
\end{enumerate}

This template is deliberately operational. A method paper can include more
theory, but a deployment report must answer the practical question: when should
the operator stop trusting the skill, and what is the cost of keeping it alive?

\section{Audit Of Claims}

\paragraph{Claim supported.}
Static initial success does not identify deployment lifetime. The formal
proposition proves this for simple exponential curves, and the full suite shows
the same issue across many nontrivial policies and drifts.

\paragraph{Claim supported.}
Threshold, horizon, censoring, and cadence can change the interpretation of a
reported lifetime. Tables~\ref{tab:threshold} and~\ref{tab:cadence} directly
support this claim.

\paragraph{Claim supported.}
Maintenance policies trade off lifetime, recovery, cost, and evaluation burden.
Tables~\ref{tab:policy-summary}, \ref{tab:cost}, and \ref{tab:shock} support
this claim.

\paragraph{Claim not made.}
The paper does not claim that the analytic half-lives transfer numerically to
real robots. The suite is a reporting stress test, not a physical deployment
measurement.

\paragraph{Claim not made.}
The paper does not claim that robot skills always decay monotonically. The
diagnostic permits nonmonotonic recovery, and the appendices explain why the
full curve must be reported.

\paragraph{Claim not made.}
The paper does not claim that a half-life scalar replaces task success,
adaptation metrics, safety metrics, or economic analysis. It claims that the
time axis must be included in reports of deployed skills.

\section{Data-Generating Process Details}

\subsection{Success Curve Components}

Each analytic success curve is built from five components. The first component
is an initial success level $s_0$ determined by the skill family and seed. The
second is a floor $s_{\min}$ that prevents the curve from becoming negative.
The third is an exponential decay term controlled by skill sensitivity, drift
rate, and policy age:
\[
S_{\text{base}}(t)=s_{\min} + (s_0-s_{\min})\exp(-\lambda_{k,d} a_p(t)).
\]
Here $k$ indexes the skill, $d$ indexes the drift process, $p$ indexes the
maintenance policy, and $a_p(t)$ is the policy-specific effective age. Frozen
skills have effective age close to calendar time. Calendar policies partly
reset effective age. Triggered and adaptive policies reduce effective age after
maintenance events. Oracle maintenance uses a privileged low effective age.

The fourth component is shock loss. Each drift process has shock days
$\tau_j$ and a shock magnitude. For $t\geq \tau_j$, the curve receives a
transient loss and a persistent loss. Maintenance policies reduce these losses
according to their shock-mitigation parameter. This creates abrupt drops in the
curve and makes recovery lag meaningful.

The fifth component is recovery and monitoring. Triggered, adaptive, repair,
and oracle policies add a delayed recovery term after shock events. Monitoring
policies add a small improvement from earlier detection. Overfit rapid
adaptation receives an early recovery benefit and a late overfit penalty. These
terms create nonmonotonic curves without changing the definition of the
threshold crossing.

\subsection{Why The Suite Uses Analytic Curves}

A hardware suite with 14 skills, 12 drift processes, 16 policies, 112 seeds,
241 days, and multiple thresholds would be expensive and slow. The analytic
suite is not a substitute for such a study. It is a way to test the reporting
logic at full scale before hardware evidence exists. Because the curves are
deterministic, every table is reproducible and every failure mode can be traced
to a known component: decay, shock, recovery, cadence, cost, or censoring.

This choice also makes the suite RAM-light. The runner does not store dense
daily trajectories for every seed. It computes compact summaries, streams seed
rows to disk, and keeps only aggregate accumulators. The represented count
therefore measures reporting checks, not memory footprint.

\subsection{Restricted Half-Life Approximation}

The runner evaluates compact sampled curves and interpolates threshold
crossings between sampled points. This is appropriate for the suite because the
goal is robust policy ranking and reporting stress, not sub-day crossing
precision. A hardware deployment should use the actual evaluation cadence and
should treat crossing time as interval-censored when checks are sparse.

\subsection{Durability Score Interpretation}

The durability score is intentionally transparent. It rewards half-life, AUC,
and uptime, and penalizes maintenance cost, evaluation cost, shock loss, and
recovery lag. It is not a universal objective. A safety-critical application
might assign much larger penalty to shock loss. A warehouse application might
assign larger penalty to downtime. A lab benchmark might ignore human labor.
The raw columns are therefore more important than the score.

\subsection{Why Multiple Thresholds Are Required}

Different thresholds ask different deployment questions. $\life{0.8}$ asks
when the policy first loses quality. $\life{0.5}$ asks when the policy has
lost half of its original success. $\life{0.3}$ asks when only a residual skill
remains. A single threshold can be defended only if the application tells us
why that threshold is operationally meaningful. The suite reports multiple
thresholds because reviewers should not have to infer this from one scalar.

\section{Scenario Case Studies}

\subsection{Peg Insertion Under Compound Drift}

Peg insertion under compound drift is the representative hard case used in
Figure~\ref{fig:representative-curves}. The skill begins with high success
because the peg, hole, and controller are calibrated. A first shock shifts the
fixture. A second shock changes compliance. A third shock appears late in the
deployment window. Frozen skill decays quickly because every shift increases
contact error. Calendar recalibration partially resets the fixture error but
can be out of phase with shocks. Sentinel and uncertainty rehearsal recover
some performance after warnings. Overfit adaptation reacts early but loses late
success. Oracle maintenance uses privileged timing and remains high. This case
illustrates why shock recovery and cadence must be reported together.

\subsection{Suction Picking Under Lighting Drift}

Suction picking under lighting and perception drift is easier. The skill starts
high, and the drift process has lower shock scale. Calendar recalibration and
domain-randomized policies look relatively strong because the main degradation
is perceptual. This case warns against overgeneralizing from one drift process.
A method that is excellent under lighting drift may not survive tactile wear or
friction changes.

\subsection{Adhesive Peeling Under Friction And Humidity Drift}

Adhesive peeling under friction and humidity drift is a hard case. Residue,
surface condition, and material response compound over time. The decay curve is
not only a sensor-calibration issue; it is a changing contact process. Simple
calendar recalibration has limited effect. Tactile servo refresh and
meta-adaptation do better because they respond to contact dynamics. Human repair
is strong because it can reset the physical setup, but its cost is high. This
case motivates including physical maintenance in the report rather than
treating all adaptation as software.

\subsection{Tool Handoff Under Human Timing Variation}

Tool handoff highlights another reporting issue: the drift process can come
from another agent. Human timing variation changes when the robot should close
the gripper, how quickly it should yield, and what safety margin is acceptable.
Static success on scripted handoffs does not predict long-horizon deployment
with changing partners. In this case, ensemble gating and active evaluation can
be useful even when they do not dominate half-life, because detecting uncertain
handoffs is operationally valuable.

\subsection{Door Handle Turning Under Fixture Pose Shift}

Door handle turning is a moderate case. Fixture pose shift can be handled by
calibration up to a point, but latch mechanics and torque requirements can
change with wear. Self-calibration and calendar recalibration help early;
adaptive methods help after repeated shifts. The case illustrates why the same
skill may require both geometric and force-based reporting.

\section{Reviewer Checklist For Half-Life Claims}

\paragraph{Check the event definition.}
Does the paper define the success threshold, the initial success estimate, and
the exact crossing rule? If not, the reported lifetime is ambiguous.

\paragraph{Check censoring.}
Does every lifetime table include a censoring flag or censoring fraction? If a
paper reports a horizon-limited value without censoring, it may be overstating
durability.

\paragraph{Check the curve.}
Is the full decay curve shown, or at least enough sampled points to understand
failure and recovery? A scalar without a curve should be treated as incomplete.

\paragraph{Check cadence.}
How often was success measured? Sparse cadence can delay crossing detection and
inflate apparent lifetime.

\paragraph{Check drift process.}
What changed during deployment? Sensor drift, object drift, fixture drift,
friction drift, and human timing drift are different stressors. A lifetime
without a named drift process is not reproducible.

\paragraph{Check maintenance.}
Was the policy frozen, recalibrated, adapted, repaired, or replaced? A
half-life number is not comparable across papers if one policy receives
maintenance and another does not.

\paragraph{Check cost.}
How much evaluation, downtime, compute, human labor, or hardware replacement was
needed? A long lifetime can be operationally weak if it requires excessive
maintenance.

\paragraph{Check negative controls.}
Does the paper include frozen, calendar, and overfit or excessive-adaptation
controls? Without negative controls, it is hard to know whether the reported
lifetime is meaningful.

\paragraph{Check threshold sensitivity.}
Does the method still look strong under a stricter threshold? If the ranking
changes sharply, the paper should explain which threshold matters for the task.

\paragraph{Check final-window behavior.}
Does the policy remain useful at the end of the window, or did it merely delay
the crossing? Day-240 success and AUC help answer this.

\paragraph{Check shocks.}
Were abrupt shifts tested? If not, the reported half-life may describe smooth
drift only.

\paragraph{Check transfer claims.}
Does the paper claim hardware durability from simulation or analytic curves?
If so, the claim should be narrowed or supported by real deployment data.
